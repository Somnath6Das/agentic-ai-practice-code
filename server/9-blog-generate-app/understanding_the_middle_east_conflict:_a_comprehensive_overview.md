## Historical Context

The roots of the Middle East conflict can be traced back to a complex interplay of colonialism, territorial division, and ideological struggle. The collapse of the **Ottoman Empire** in World War I (1914–1918) marked the beginning of modern tensions. The empire, which had ruled much of the region for centuries, was dismantled by the **Sykes-Picot Agreement (1916)**, a secret British-French deal that partitioned Ottoman territories into spheres of influence, ignoring local populations and laying the groundwork for artificial borders that persist today.

Following the war, the League of Nations granted Britain control over **Mandatory Palestine** (1920–1948), a territory that became a focal point of conflict. British policies during this period were contradictory: they supported both Zionist aspirations for a Jewish homeland and Arab nationalist movements. The **Balfour Declaration (1917)**, which endorsed a "national home for the Jewish people" in Palestine, clashed with earlier promises to Arab leaders who had fought for independence from Ottoman rule. This duality fueled distrust and set the stage for future conflict.

The **1948 Arab-Israeli War** was a pivotal moment. After the United Nations proposed a **Partition Plan (1947)** to divide Palestine into Jewish and Arab states, the Jewish Agency accepted the proposal, but Arab leaders rejected it. When Israel declared independence in May 1948, neighboring Arab states (Egypt, Jordan, Syria, and Iraq) invaded, leading to a war that resulted in Israel's victory and the displacement of hundreds of thousands of Palestinians—the **Nakba** (catastrophe) in Palestinian memory. This unresolved refugee crisis and contested land claims remain central to the conflict today.

These historical events—colonial division, competing nationalisms, and the trauma of 1948—created a framework of mistrust and unresolved grievances that continue to shape the Middle East's geopolitical landscape.

The Middle East conflict involves a complex web of regional and global actors, each with distinct interests, alliances, and historical grievances.

Established in 1948, Israel’s primary objective has been to ensure its security and sovereignty. It maintains a strong military and has engaged in multiple wars with Arab states. ### **Palestine**

- **Iran-Saudi Rivalry**: This sectarian and geopolitical clash extends to Yemen (Houthis vs. Saudi-led coalition), Syria (Iran-backed militias vs. Saudi-supported factions), and Lebanon (Hezbollah’s ties to Iran).

### **2. Economic Impact: Energy and Markets**

- **OPEC+ and Strategic Reserves**: Saudi Arabia’s leadership in OPEC+ allows it to control production levels, balancing supply with global demand and mitigating economic shocks.

# Peace Efforts and Challenges

The Middle East conflict has seen numerous peace initiatives aimed at resolving decades of tension between Israel, Palestine, and other regional actors. These efforts highlight both the potential for diplomacy and the deep-rooted challenges that persist.

### Historical Peace Agreements

1. **Camp David Accords (1978)**  
   Negotiated between Egypt and Israel with U.S. mediation, the Camp David Accords marked a historic breakthrough. Egypt became the first Arab state to formally recognize Israel in exchange for Israeli withdrawal from the Sinai Peninsula. This agreement led to lasting peace between the two nations but did not resolve the Israeli-Palestinian conflict, as many Arab states refused to support it.

2. **Oslo Accords (1993)**  
   The Oslo Accords, signed by Israel and the Palestine Liberation Organization (PLO), established a framework for Palestinian self-governance in parts of the West Bank and Gaza Strip. While they were a symbolic milestone, the agreements failed to address core issues like borders, Jerusalem, and Palestinian refugees. Violence, such as the Second Intifada (2000–2005), and continued Israeli settlement expansion undermined progress.

3. **Abraham Accords (2020)**  
   These agreements, brokered by the U.S., normalized relations between Israel and several Arab states, including the UAE, Bahrain, and Sudan. While expanding diplomatic ties and economic cooperation, the accords excluded Palestinian participation, drawing criticism for sidelining their plight.

### Current Peace Efforts  
Today, efforts remain fragmented. The Israeli government under Benjamin Netanyahu has prioritized domestic political stability over concessions, while Palestinian leadership faces division between Fatah (governing in the West Bank) and Hamas (ruling in Gaza). Regional dynamics, including Iran’s influence and Saudi-Israeli normalization talks, add complexity. The U.S., EU, and other global powers continue to advocate for a two-state solution, though funding for Palestinian institutions has dwindled under shifting political priorities.

### Key Obstacles  
- **Settlement Expansion**: Israeli settlements in the West Bank are considered illegal under international law and a major barrier to a two-state solution.  
- **Security Distrust**: Israelis fear Palestinian statehood could threaten their security, while Palestinians view Israeli military occupation as a violation of their rights.  
- **Regional Tensions**: Conflicts in Syria, Yemen, and Iran-Israel proxy wars divert attention from peace negotiations.  
- **Internal Politics**: Hardline factions on both sides often oppose compromises, fearing backlash from supporters.  

Despite these challenges, moments of diplomacy—such as the 2023 Gaza ceasefire brokered by Egypt and Qatar—show that dialogue remains possible. A lasting resolution, however, requires addressing not only territorial disputes but also the emotional and historical wounds that define this enduring conflict.
